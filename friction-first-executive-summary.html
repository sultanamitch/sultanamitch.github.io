Executive Summary

Friction-First Earnings Screener

20 companies · 3 years of earnings data · 3 company groups · yfinance + SQLite

Overview

Does removing friction actually compound?

The thesis going in was simple: companies whose core product is removing friction from business processes — payments, data infrastructure, workflow automation — should structurally outperform legacy tech over time. I wanted to test that against actual earnings data rather than just assert it.

I pulled three years of financials (2022–2025) across 20 companies split into three groups: a friction-first core of 11 companies including Visa, Mastercard, Snowflake, Palantir, and Nvidia; a legacy control group of IBM, Oracle, Intel, and HP; and a personal watchlist of BlackBerry, Nokia, Ericsson, Microsoft, and Google.

The gap was wider than I expected — and the legacy group's 2024 EPS contraction tells a specific story about what happens when cost-cutting runs out of road. One data limitation worth flagging: Intel's 2025 numbers include an $8.9B CHIPS Act injection, which partially masks organic deterioration in the legacy group's EPS trend.

Takeaways

What the data supports.

  • 01
    The friction-first thesis holds on the fundamentals Revenue growth, gross margin, and EPS trajectory all favor friction-first companies over the legacy control group across the full period. This isn't a valuation call — it's a business model observation. Companies that reduce transaction costs tend to grow into their TAM rather than defend against it.
  • 02
    Legacy EPS recovery in 2025 is partly artificial Intel's CHIPS Act capital injection inflates the legacy group's 2025 EPS stabilization. Strip that out and the organic picture is worse. Cost-cutting-driven EPS has a ceiling — once you've cut, you have to grow, and the legacy group's revenue lines don't support it.
  • 03
    Three years is a short window for a structural thesis The 2022–2025 period captures a specific rate environment and post-COVID normalization. A friction-first screener run across a full cycle — including a downturn — would be a more complete test of whether the margin advantage is structural or cyclical.
